The build

How we built it — end to end

We built this end to end: frontend, backend, database, and AI, working together as one product.

The data is real — 8,680 restaurants across 9 Indian cities, ingested into Supabase (our database) with menus, ratings, and each user's order history.

The intelligence is Gemini, connected through its API. It reads a user's order history and writes their personalised homepage — the sections, the picks, the reasons.

The honesty rule — before every request, the backend pulls a shortlist of real restaurants from our database, and Gemini may only recommend from that list. It cannot invent a place. (The technique is called RAG.)

The logic

How we identify who you are

A user's order history is just a list: which restaurant, which dish, when. From it we calculate three simple numbers:

Repeat — how often they re-order the same dish from the same place (repeated orders ÷ total orders)

Variety — at places they visit often, how many different dishes they try (different dishes ÷ visits)

Explore — how many different restaurants they try overall (different restaurants ÷ total orders)

Whichever number is highest names the personality. No AI involved in this step — pure arithmetic anyone can verify.

Meet the three eaters

Pick a persona — see their personalised homepage

The business goal behind all three: move users up the ladder Loyalist → Explorer → Variety Seeker, because exploration drives incremental orders. Now pick a persona — everything below the tabs belongs to your selection:

2A — The Loyalist, walked through

Who: orders the same dish from the same place, again and again.

Their homepage: their restaurant first, showing dishes there they haven't tried.

Goal: deepen trust, widen the order.

Take Kavitha: 8 of her 10 orders are Chicken Biryani from Ss Hyderabad Biryani. Her Repeat number towers over the other two — she's a Loyalist.

The engine finds her favourite restaurant, notes every dish she's already tried there, and asks Gemini one focused question: from this restaurant's real menu, recommend the highest-rated dishes she hasn't tried yet.

That becomes the first thing she sees: her trusted place, with something new inside it.

The rest of her homepage

Top Rated Near You — eight well-rated places around her, rating badge and delivery time on every card; View all sorts by ★ Top Rated or ⚡ Fastest.

Her persona signature — a delivery scooter waiting at the kerb. The moment she orders, it rides toward her home, timed to her order's actual delivery ETA.

2B — The Explorer, walked through

Who: two or three trusted restaurants, but a new dish every time.

Their homepage: new restaurants that match their proven cuisines.

Goal: same taste, new places.

Take Priya: 12 orders, but only 3 restaurants — and never the same dish twice. Her Variety number wins — she's an Explorer. Her loyalty is to a taste, not a menu item.

The engine reads her top cuisines — Fast Food, North Indian, Chinese — and asks Gemini: from well-rated places she has never ordered from, recommend the ones matching these cuisines.

That becomes the first thing she sees: new places, proven taste.

The rest of her homepage

Trending Near You — a section Loyalists don't get. A second AI agent searches the live web for what India is eating right now, then verifies every dish is actually orderable in her city at a well-rated place. Cards carry the best-rated option's numbers; "At 5 places →" opens them all, best first.

Top Rated Near You — as on every homepage: eight well-rated places, numbers upfront.

Her persona signature — a hungry runner forever chasing a bouncing momo 🥟 — always after the next great bite. When she orders, the scooter takes over, timed to her ETA.

2C — The Variety Seeker, walked through

Who: a different restaurant nearly every order.

Their homepage: the best of right now — meal-slot aware, taste barely a factor.

Goal: pure discovery.

Take Vikram: 12 orders, 12 different restaurants. His Explore number is a perfect 1.0 — a Variety Seeker. Loyal to the platform, not to any kitchen.

For him the engine barely reads taste history. It reads the clock: it's dinner time, so it asks Gemini — what are the best dinner options in this city right now? Quality first, breadth over familiarity.

He sees tonight's cravings — Biryani, Pizza, Kebabs and more — and View all opens a grid of ~20 food items for the current meal slot. At breakfast, it rebuilds around dosas and chai.

The rest of his homepage

Trending Near You — same live engine as the Explorer's: web-trending dishes, verified orderable in his city at well-rated places.

Top Rated Near You — as on every homepage: eight well-rated places, numbers upfront.

His persona signature — a craving roulette: a wheel of cuisines spins, lands, announces "Pizza it is! 🍕", rests, and spins again. When he orders, the scooter takes over, timed to his ETA.

The UX journey

Interface decisions, from user psychology

A clean canvas for quick decisions — the home stays white and uncluttered, so photos, ratings, and delivery times do the talking.

Controls that behave the way they look — the Food · Instamart · Dineout · Scenes control looks like a slider, so it is one: drag the pill and it follows your finger. The VEG toggle flips the page's mode in one tap.

A scroll never gets stuck — every row carries 8 cards, with a soft edge-fade hinting there's more, and a consistent View all in every section's corner.

No dead ends — slide to Instamart and instead of a blank page, a playful "still being cooked 🧑‍🍳" note appears and guides you back. Unbuilt tabs look disabled; the back button always returns exactly where you came from; an empty cart offers "Explore food →".

What's next

Where this goes from here

Learn from taps, not just orders — today the persona comes from order history; next, what users browse but don't order refines it.

Personas that migrate — as a Loyalist starts exploring, their homepage should notice and shift with them.

Prove the ladder — A/B test the core thesis: do personalised homepages actually move people up Loyalist → Explorer → Variety Seeker?

Candour

What's real, what's simulated

The restaurant base is a real public dataset — names, cities, ratings, cuisines, delivery times. Dish menus and user order histories are synthesised on top of it. A scoped demonstration — at platform scale it runs on real order data.
