How it works

Four steps, one honest trick

No training, no fine-tuning, no black box. Behaviour is scored with plain arithmetic; the LLM only ranks, assembles, and explains — it's never allowed to invent.

1

Score

Order history → three behaviour dials. The dominant dial picks the strategy.

2

Retrieve

RAG filters 8,680 real restaurants (86,800 dishes, 9 cities) to ~15 grounded candidates.

3

Generate

Gemini assembles the homepage as strict JSON — a reason on every item.

4

Serve

Facts re-verified against the database; cached 30 min per user per meal slot.

repeat  = orders repeating a (restaurant, dish) pair ÷ total  // the usual
variety = distinct dishes ÷ visits, where visits ≥ 2        // loyal but curious
explore = distinct restaurants ÷ total orders            // never the same place
persona = argmax(normalise(repeat, variety, explore))

Decisions I'd defend

The judgment calls behind it

Why LLM + RAG

Rules can't reason; an ungrounded LLM invents restaurants. Retrieval feeds it only real, orderable places — grounded and smart.

Degrade, never break

Model waterfall with retries; if all models are busy, a handsome non-AI homepage ships instead of an error.

Facts stay verified

The AI writes the story; the database overwrites the numbers — ratings and delivery times are never the model's word.

Diet is sacred

A declared diet is an unbreakable constraint. Jain and vegan are never guessed — guessing wrong is real harm.

Location is consent

Listings need live GPS permission — no quiet fallback to a stored address you may not be at.

Cost by design

30-min homepage cache per user per slot; 24-hour trend cache. AI cost scales with people, not page loads.

Deep dive · 02

Quantifying quality on every card

Every number a user sees follows three rules: it must be real (re-verified from the database, never the model's word), it must be readable ("25–30 min", never "28m"), and it must be worth showing — the homepage carries nothing rated below ★4.0.

—The green tells you how safe the bet is

Rating badges deepen with quality — a five-step ramp from lightest green at ★4.0 to darkest at ★5.0, consistent on every card, list, and page. A row becomes scannable without reading a single number:

★ 4.0 ★ 4.3 ★ 4.5 ★ 4.7 ★ 5.0

—The gate has one honest exception

Sub-4.0 restaurants aren't erased — they live behind search and inside their own pages. And a dish with an ordinary rating still appears inside its restaurant's menu; it just never gets homepage real estate. The homepage is a showcase, not a directory.

Deep dive · 03

The trending engine, end to end

1

Search the real web

A second Gemini agent with Google Search grounding finds what's viral across India — constrained to specific, orderable, family-friendly dishes (1–3 words, no categories).

2

Cache nationally

One India-wide list, refreshed every 24 hours — trending is national; availability is local.

3

Match to menus

Each dish is matched against 86,800 real menu items in the user's city (★4.0+ kitchens only, one entry per restaurant).

4

Gate & show

The section renders only when ≥4 trending dishes are genuinely orderable nearby. No inventory, no section.

The multi-restaurant answer: when Chicken Biryani is served at five places, the card carries the best-rated match's rating and ETA — a real restaurant's numbers, never an average — and says "At 5 places →". Tapping opens all five, best first. And there are no "#1 / #2" ranks: the visible list is availability-filtered, so numbering it would imply a national order that isn't true. One uniform 🔥 badge; the red pulse marks genuinely viral search signals.

The performance story: login originally waited 5–10s for live generation. Switching to stale-while-revalidate — serve the last page instantly, refresh silently in the background — brought measured login time to ~0.7s, while pages still stay at most one visit old.

Deep dive · 05

The UX ideology — psychology first

Every interface decision came from asking what the user's mind expects, not what was easy to build.

01If it looks like a slider, it must slide

The Food · Instamart · Dineout · Scenes control is a pill in a track — visually, that's a slider, and users' fingers treat it as one. Tap-only handling broke that expectation. So the pill genuinely drags under the finger and snaps to the nearest tab. Affordance honoured, no manual needed.

FoodInstamartDineoutScenes

And when a user slides into an unbuilt vertical, there's no dead screen: the feed blurs below the nav (the toggle stays sharp and usable), a friendly "still being cooked 🧑‍🍳" sheet appears with per-vertical copy, hopping between verticals restarts the message, and after 5 idle seconds the pill glides home on its own.

02Identity is sacred: a restaurant is not a dish

Cards never mix identities. A restaurant card shows a venue photo (interior/storefront — unique within its row), the restaurant's rating, and no + button — you can't add a restaurant to a cart. A dish card shows the dish's photo and its + button. Prices appear only where a purchase is one tap away (the Loyalist hero). Ratings always belong to the thing that was rated.

03A scroll must never die halfway

Users flick a row expecting momentum; four items kill it. Every carousel carries 8 items, a right-edge fade whispers "there's more", every section's View all sits in the same top-right spot and opens that section's own list — never a keyword dump. AI-written blurbs are capped at 4 words: decision info, not paragraphs.

04No dead ends, no fake doors

Unbuilt things look unbuilt: bottom tabs and the bell are visibly disabled, not booby-trapped. The back button always returns exactly where you came from — home, a list, search, or a dish page — never through a screen you didn't visit. An empty cart isn't a dead grey button; it's a warm state with an "Explore food →" way forward.

05Delight in small doses — and personalised too

Even the easter eggs follow the persona thesis: the Loyalist's footer has a delivery scooter waiting at the kerb; the Explorer's has a runner forever chasing a bouncing momo; the Variety Seeker gets a craving roulette that spins, lands ("Pizza it is! 🍕"), and spins again. Place an order and everyone gets the scooter — riding toward the house over the real 30-minute ETA, its caption counting down. Peak-end rule, applied.

Shipped like a product, not a prototype: the build went through 10 rounds of user-review iteration. Reviews caught real flaws — "#4 Trending" implying a false national rank, prices cluttering discovery, dish photos on restaurant cards — and each fix became one of the rules above. The process is the point: decisions here are argued from user psychology, tested on a live build, and revised on evidence.
